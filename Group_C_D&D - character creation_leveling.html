Leveling


Here's an introduction on leveling your character

Starting above first level

Sometimes you'll begin your adventure at a higher level

Joining an existing group

More often than not, you'll be introduced to D&D through someone who has been playing a campaign that invites you to join. When you join an existing game, you'll often start at a similar level to everyone else as to not fall behind everyone out of the gate and to have difficulty with the encounters that they'll be facing. It's more enjoyable for you and your friends if your character has the same impact as everyone else in battle.

From a roleplaying perspective

While writing your backstory, if your character does get to start above first level, it's assumed that they've done some adventuring prior to joining their party. You should consider this in your backstory when you're telling everyone about the previous events you experienced before you encountered them. How to level

Some information on leveling your character

For each level

Each time your character levels, they'll earn additional hitpoints by rolling their hit die and adding their consitution modifier. They'll also gain addional features based on their class including abilities, upgrades to their class like spell slots, an adjustment to their proficiency bonus, and every fourth level they gain two additional points to assign to their ability scores.



Follow the chart

Each class has a chart like the one pictured in the player's handbook to help walk you through the advancement of your class. The chart keeps things easy to follow based on your character level what your proficiency bonus should be, what features you have unlocked, and how many spell slots you have/how strong your class ability is. Just make sure you follow your class chart and go through each level individually!
